# Adults' Experiences and Perceptions of Health Apps and Wearables

**Module:** SPH5409 Qualitative Methods in Public Health, NUS Saw Swee Hock School of Public Health  
**Date:** November 2025

---

## Overview

A qualitative thematic analysis exploring how adults in Singapore adopt and sustain use of health trackers and wearables. Five in-depth interviews were conducted and analysed using a hybrid deductive-inductive approach, with Atlas.ti v23 supporting the coding process.

---

## Research Question

What are adults' experiences and perceptions of using health apps and wearables — specifically, what motivates initial adoption and sustained use, what are the barriers to continued engagement, and how do motivations change over time across different user profiles?

---

## Methods

| Item | Detail |
|------|--------|
| Design | Qualitative thematic analysis |
| Approach | Hybrid deductive-inductive (Fereday & Muir-Cochrane, 2006) |
| Sample | n=5, purposive sampling, Singapore adults |
| Data collection | Semi-structured interviews, 27–38 min (Sep–Oct 2025) |
| Analysis software | Atlas.ti v23 |
| Reporting standard | COREQ 32-item checklist (Tong et al., 2007) |

---

## Key Findings

Five motivational domains emerged:

1. **Extrinsic incentives** — financial rewards and insurance subsidies drove initial adoption
2. **Gamified goals** — activity rings and progress bars sustained day-to-day engagement
3. **Social motivation** — peer comparison and communal activity created interpersonal accountability
4. **Self-quantification** — curiosity about personal data and performance monitoring
5. **Barriers** — active barriers (manual data entry) caused abandonment; passive barriers (inaccuracy) were tolerated

**Cross-cutting finding:** Motivations evolved from extrinsic rewards toward habitual, automatic behaviour over time. Trackers amplified pre-existing motivation rather than creating new motivation — functioning as optimisation tools for already-active users rather than behaviour change interventions for sedentary populations.

## Limitations

- Small sample (n=5) limits generalisability, though thematic saturation was achieved
- Discontinued users were underrepresented
- Singapore-specific incentive context (Healthier SG) may not transfer to other settings
- Recall bias possible for retrospective accounts of early adoption experiences
